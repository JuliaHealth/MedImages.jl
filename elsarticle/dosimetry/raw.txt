Discuss what these sources say about NURBS Hybrid Phantoms, in the larger context of Computational Phantoms.

Computational phantoms are mathematical representations of the human body used in dosimetry to model organs and the whole body for calculating absorbed radiation doses
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}. Historically, dosimetry relied on standardized, stylized phantoms consisting of simple geometric shapes (such as the Christy-Eckerman phantoms)
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}. While easy to use, these traditional models assume a standardized individual with homogeneous tissue and uniform activity distribution within organs, failing to capture patient-specific anatomy, organ mass variations, and heterogeneous radiotracer uptake
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}

 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}.
To address these limitations, dosimetry models have evolved to utilize Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines (NURBS)
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}

 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. These models act as hybrid computational phantoms because they bridge the gap between simple stylized models and rigid voxel-based models: they combine the high-resolution anatomical reality of voxel phantoms with the mathematical flexibility of stylized phantoms
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}.
The sources highlight several key characteristics and benefits of NURBS hybrid phantoms:
Improved Anatomical Realism: NURBS phantoms provide a much better anatomical representation than older stylized models. For instance, they accurately model tissues that are physically in contact with each other
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}. One study comparing an older stylized phantom (OLINDA/EXM v. 1.0) with a NURBS computational phantom (OLINDA/EXM v. 2.0) found a 6.37\\\\% difference in absorbed dose estimates, which was attributed to this superior anatomical representation
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}.
Deformability and Mass Scaling: Because they are mathematically defined, NURBS phantoms can be easily deformed and scaled
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}. They can be modified to match standard organ masses, such as those defined in the International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) Publication 89
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. This flexibility allows clinicians to derive patient weight- and age-specific S-values, facilitating highly personalized dosimetry across different patient populations
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}.
Automated Patient-Specific Morphing: In advanced 3D dosimetry applications like VIDA (Voxel-Based Internal Dosimetry Application), NURBS phantoms can be rendered directly over a patient's CT scans
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. Custom transformation algorithms are used to morph the phantom's body contour and organ surfaces to precisely match the individual patient's anatomy
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. The deformed NURBS model is then converted into a voxelized format matching the resolution of the functional SPECT or PET images, which saves significant time by bypassing the need for manual organ segmentation
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}.
Integration into Modern Software: NURBS technology is the foundation of modern standard dosimetry software. For example, OLINDA/EXM version 2.0 utilizes a hybrid computational phantom based on NURBS technology, specifically adapting the RADAR reference adult male NURBS phantom (a modified version of the NCAT cardiac-torso phantom)
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}

 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}.
Preclinical Applications: The flexibility of NURBS is also heavily utilized in small animal dosimetry. The 4D digital mouse body (MOBY) and rat body (ROBY) phantoms rely on NURBS mathematical models to define precise organ boundaries for Monte Carlo simulations in preclinical research
 \cite{GuptaLee2020}.
Discuss what these sources say about Voxel-Based Dosimetry, in the larger context of Dosimetry Calculation Methods.

Historically, radiation dosimetry in nuclear medicine relied on the Medical Internal Radiation Dose (MIRD) schema, which estimates mean absorbed doses at the whole-organ level
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}. This traditional method assumes a homogeneous activity distribution and uniform tissue density within standard, stylized phantom geometries, failing to account for patient-specific anatomical variations, actual tumor sizes, or heterogeneous disease distributions
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. To overcome these limitations and move towards personalized medicine in targeted radiopharmaceutical therapy (TRT), voxel-based dosimetry has been developed. By dividing the patient's anatomy into microscopic 3D volumetric elements (voxels) utilizing hybrid imaging like SPECT/CT or PET/CT, this approach provides a high-resolution, patient-specific 3D map of radiation dose
 \cite{GtzLang2021}

 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}.
Within voxel-based dosimetry, several distinct calculation methods offer varying trade-offs between accuracy and computational speed:
Direct Monte Carlo (MC) Simulations: Widely considered the gold standard for voxel-based dosimetry, MC explicitly models the transport of individual photons and charged particles through heterogeneous tissues derived from a patient's CT scan
 \cite{GtzLang2021}

 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}. While it provides the highest accuracy by capturing complex radiation-matter interactions at tissue boundaries, it is exceptionally computationally expensive and time-consuming (often taking hours to days), historically limiting its routine clinical use
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}

 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}.
Dose Voxel Kernels (DVK) and Voxel S-Values (VSV): This approach calculates dose by convolving a time-integrated activity (TIA) map with precalculated kernels representing the dose deposited in target voxels per radioactive decay in a source voxel
 \cite{PistoneAuditore2022}

 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}. While much faster than MC simulations because it avoids complex radiation transport calculations, standard VSV methods assume an infinite, uniform medium (usually soft tissue or water)
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{GuptaLee2020}. This can introduce significant errors at tissue interfaces like bone or lung, though advanced scaling techniques can apply voxel-by-voxel density corrections using CT data to mitigate this
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{MahmoudiPirayesh2021}.
Dose Point Kernel (DPK) Convolution: Conceptually similar to DVKs, DPKs represent the continuous radial distribution of absorbed dose around an isotropic point source
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}. They are often discretized and convolved via fast Fourier transforms but face similar limitations regarding tissue heterogeneity unless appropriately scaled
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}

 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}.
Local Deposition Method (LDM): The simplest voxel-based approximation assumes that all emitted energy is absorbed entirely within the source voxel
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. While fast and somewhat appropriate for short-range beta emitters like 177Lu, it ignores cross-dose from neighboring voxels and the highly penetrating photon emissions that also contribute to total absorbed dose
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}
 \cite{ReschFouladgar2023}.
Advanced and Emerging Solvers: To bridge the gap between the accuracy of MC and the speed of kernel convolutions, newer algorithms have been adapted for TRT. These include Linear Boltzmann Transport Equation (LBTE) solvers (e.g., Acuros MRT or GBBS), which solve transport equations deterministically in heterogeneous media in a fraction of the time
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}

 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}. Other approaches include Collapsed-Cone Superposition (CCS), which scales DPKs dynamically based on tissue densities
 \cite{TerroPerret2025}
 \cite{TerroPerret2025}, and Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), which are trained on MC data to predict voxel-level doses or specific DVKs directly from imaging data almost instantaneously
 \cite{GtzLang2021}

 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}.
The primary advantage of voxel-based dosimetry is its ability to reveal spatial non-uniformities in dose deposition that whole-organ averages obscure
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}. This allows for the generation of Dose-Volume Histograms (DVHs) and Dose-Mass Histograms (DMHs), which are critical for evaluating tumor control probabilities and normal-tissue complication probabilities, bringing TRT closer to the standard of care seen in external beam radiotherapy
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}

 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. By calculating precise doses to highly active tumor sub-regions and evaluating dose gradients in critical organs like the kidneys or bone marrow, clinicians can personalize administered activities, thereby optimizing therapeutic efficacy while sparing healthy tissue
 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}

 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}.
Despite its superiority over organ-level methods, voxel-based dosimetry faces several challenges. Its absolute accuracy is fundamentally limited by the spatial resolution and noise of the underlying SPECT or PET images
 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}

 \cite{ReschFouladgar2023}
 \cite{MulyadiJung2022}. The Partial Volume Effect (PVE) causes significant image blurring, leading to underestimations of absorbed dose in small structures or tumors
 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}

 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}
 \cite{MulyadiJung2022}. Furthermore, the full voxel-dosimetry workflow requires sequential imaging, accurate rigid or deformable image registration across multiple time points, and complex curve fitting, which can be burdensome for both patients and clinical staff
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}

 \cite{DelkerFendler2015}
 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}.
Discuss what these sources say about Tumour Sink Effect, in the larger context of Pharmacokinetic (PK) Modelling.

The tumour sink effect describes the phenomenon where large tumor masses (a high total tumor volume or burden) significantly influence the pharmacokinetics of radiopharmaceuticals by acting as "antigen sinks"
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}. Because these large tumor masses contain a vast number of specific receptor binding sites, they absorb a massive proportion of the administered radioligand
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}.
This effect has major implications for how radiation is distributed throughout the body during targeted radionuclide therapy (TRT):
Impact on Healthy Organs: A high tumor burden draws the radiopharmaceutical away from healthy tissues, which can lead to decreased activity concentrations and lower radiation-absorbed doses in dose-limiting organs at risk (OARs), such as the kidneys and salivary glands
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}
 \cite{XueGafita2022}. Conversely, there have been concerns that patients with low-volume disease might suffer from unfavorable radioligand distribution, leading to higher toxicity in normal organs because the "sink" is absent
 \cite{PetersPriv2021}. However, recent studies suggest that the lack of a tumor sink effect in low-volume disease may actually be of less concern than expected, as the tumor-to-organ uptake ratio can remain favorable
 \cite{PetersPriv2021}.
Impact on the Tumor: While the sink effect protects healthy tissue, it also disperses the radioligand across a massive volume of cancer cells. This dispersion carries the risk of decreasing the activity concentration within the tumor itself, potentially resulting in tumor underdosing and a less effective treatment
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}.
The Role of Pharmacokinetic (PK) Modelling Because the tumour sink effect creates high interpatient pharmacokinetic variability, the standard "one-activity-fits-all" approach to administering radiopharmaceuticals is often suboptimal
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}. To account for this variability, Physiologically Based Pharmacokinetic (PBPK) models are highly suitable because they can explicitly model both the patient's individual organ physiology and the tumor's pathophysiology
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}.
In the context of the tumour sink effect, PBPK modeling provides several critical insights:
Quantitative Simulation of Tumor Volume: PBPK models systematically investigate the sink effect by simulating how different Total Tumor Volumes (TTV) impact the biologically effective doses (BEDs) delivered to the tumor and OARs
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}.
Treatment Individualization: PBPK models have demonstrated that patients with a large, PSMA-positive tumor mass can safely be administered higher activities and higher peptide amounts of the radiopharmaceutical
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}. Because the tumor sink effect protects their healthy organs, the therapy can be personalized to maximize the radiation dose to the tumor without exceeding the critical toxicity limits for the OARs
 \cite{XueGafita2022}
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}.
Dynamic Adaptation to Disease Progression: As a patient undergoes therapy, the disease may progress or regress, which fundamentally changes the tumor volume and the number of specific binding sites available
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}. This means the intensity of the tumour sink effect will shift over time, altering the patient's biodistribution and renal clearance
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}. PK modeling allows clinicians to track these spatiotemporal changes and extrapolate them to adapt therapeutic doses cycle by cycle
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}
 \cite{XueGafita2022}.
Discuss what these sources say about Averaging Strategies, in the larger context of Dosimetry Calculation Methods.

In radiopharmaceutical therapy, dosimetry calculation methods range from global, organ-level approaches to highly personalized, voxel-level calculations. Traditional organ-level dosimetry (such as the standard MIRD formalism) utilizes mathematical phantoms to estimate the mean absorbed dose across whole organs, assuming uniform activity and homogeneous tissue density
 \cite{GtzLang2021},
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021},
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}. In contrast, advanced voxel-based dosimetry—including dose voxel kernels (DVK) and direct Monte Carlo (MC) simulations—divides the patient's anatomy into microscopic 3D volumes (voxels) to account for patient-specific tissue heterogeneities and non-uniform distributions of radioactivity
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024},
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019},
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}.
Even when employing high-resolution voxel-based calculations, clinical practice frequently requires aggregating these voxel-wise doses into scalar averages to evaluate entire organs, paired organs (like kidneys), or multiple tumor lesions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. The sources identify that the specific averaging strategies used to aggregate these doses can drastically alter the final reported values, leading to significant inconsistencies if not properly defined
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
The sources categorize averaging strategies into two primary domains: averaging within a specific region and averaging between multiple regions.
Averaging Within Regions When calculating the average absorbed dose inside a single volume of interest (such as a single organ or tumor), two primary definitions are used:
Density-weighted region-average: This method weights the point-wise absorbed doses by the mass density of the tissue, effectively dividing the total absorbed energy by the total mass of the region
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. The sources strongly endorse this strategy because it aligns with the formal ICRU definition of mean absorbed dose and is mathematically consistent with the MIRD formalism
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Region-average (unweighted): This is a simple spatial average across the region's volume, regardless of tissue density
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
While both strategies yield identical results in perfectly homogeneous tissues, they diverge significantly when applied to regions with markedly non-uniform mass densities (such as tumors situated near bone or lung interfaces)
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Furthermore, these distinct strategies correlate with different graphical representations of dose distribution: the unweighted region-average corresponds to traditional Dose-Volume Histograms (DVHs), whereas the density-weighted average corresponds to Dose-Mass Histograms (DMHs)
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. DMHs are argued to be a much more physically meaningful way to represent absorbed-dose non-uniformity
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Averaging Between Regions When calculating a single mean absorbed dose across anatomically separated regions—such as combining the left and right kidneys or evaluating multiple metastatic tumors—the sources identify three averaging strategies:
Mass-weighted average: This combines the total absorbed energy of all sub-regions and divides it by their combined mass. It is logically coherent to pair this with the density-weighted average used within individual regions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Volume-weighted average: Averages the regions based on their respective physical volumes
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Unweighted average: This strategy averages the mean doses of the individual regions equally, entirely disregarding their differences in size or mass
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
The choice of strategy between regions can heavily skew dosimetric results. For example, mass-weighted and volume-weighted averages generally yield similar outcomes, but an unweighted average can drastically understate or overstate the true dose
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. If a patient has multiple tumors where one massive lesion accounts for 70\\\\% of the total tumor burden, an unweighted average gives a tiny adjacent lesion the exact same mathematical influence as the massive one, potentially reducing the reported average dose rate by up to 50\\\\%
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Conclusion Because these varying strategies—whether within a single organ or across multiple lesions—can lead to substantially different dosimetric estimates, it is crucial to explicitly specify the averaging strategy being applied
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. When comparing different dosimetry software platforms or establishing dose-toxicity thresholds, failure to apply consistent averaging strategies can result in highly misleading conclusions that obscure the true physical and biological effects of the therapy
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023},
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Discuss what these sources say about Population PKPD Models, in the larger context of Pharmacokinetic (PK) Modelling.

Population Pharmacokinetic (PK) models use a collective approach to describe the biokinetics of radiopharmaceuticals by simultaneously fitting data from an entire population of patients, rather than fitting data for each individual separately
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}. In traditional PK modelling for internal dosimetry, estimating the time-integrated activity (TIA) of an organ or tumor usually involves fitting mathematical functions (like a sum of exponentials) to individual patient data
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}
 \cite{HardiansyahKletting2020}. However, this individual approach is highly sensitive to noise and requires dense sampling—meaning patients must undergo multiple burdensome imaging sessions over several days
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}

 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}
 \cite{HardiansyahKletting2020}.
By utilizing Nonlinear Mixed-Effects Models (NLMEMs), population PK modelling overcomes these limitations because it does not require densely sampled or comprehensive data from every single patient
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}. This approach can successfully disentangle residual unexplained variability (such as image noise) from interindividual variability (IIV) and interoccasion or cycle-to-cycle variability (IOV)
 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}. Because it draws on population-wide pharmacokinetic trends, NLMEM allows for highly accurate "Single-Time-Point" (STP) dosimetry, drastically reducing the number of scans a patient needs
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}.
In the larger context of PK modelling, population models can be structured as Physiologically Based Pharmacokinetic (PBPK) models
 \cite{BegumThieme2018}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. While standard exponential fits lack physical meaning, PBPK models incorporate anatomically and physiologically relevant mechanisms, such as blood flow, receptor density, cellular internalization, and physical decay
 \cite{HardiansyahKletting2020}

 \cite{BegumThieme2018}
 \cite{HardiansyahKletting2020}. When combined with a population approach, these models can be designed "top-down," estimating biological parameters and their variances directly from clinical data rather than relying strictly on predefined assumptions
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
To bridge the gap between radiation exposure and actual treatment outcomes, population PK models are expanded into Pharmacokinetic/Pharmacodynamic (PKPD) models
 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. This is achieved by adding a sequential pharmacodynamic (PD) compartment to the model to represent a biomarker of disease response over time
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. For example, in the treatment of metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer with 177Lu-PSMA, the PD compartment tracks individual Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) dynamics during and after treatment
 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
The sources highlight several major insights provided by Population PKPD models:
Quantifying the Exposure-Response Relationship: PKPD models link the estimated radiopharmaceutical concentration in the tumor directly to PSA changes, proving that higher cumulative absorbed doses yield better treatment responses
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Direct and Delayed Treatment Effects: PKPD analysis reveals that tumor response is governed by both immediate (direct) and delayed effects of the radiation, which is characteristic of late-responding tissues with low alpha/beta ratios
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Identifying Cycle Effects: These population models can quantify structural changes over the course of treatment, such as a significant decline in tumor uptake during subsequent therapy cycles (e.g., dropping to 73\\\\%, 50\\\\%, and 44\\\\% in cycles 2, 3, and 4–7 compared to cycle 1). This is likely caused by radiation-induced cell death or a decrease in receptor expression
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Ultimately, the development of Population PKPD models forms the basis for personalized precision dosing via Bayesian forecasting
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. Once population parameters and variabilities are established, a patient's baseline characteristics (like initial PSA and tumor volume) and limited early imaging data can be used to predict their specific tumor exposure, expected PSA response, and potential organ toxicity
 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. This enables a therapeutic drug monitoring approach, moving away from "one-size-fits-all" fixed activities toward individualized dose adjustments that maximize tumor destruction while ensuring patient safety
 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}

 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Discuss what these sources say about Anthropomorphic Stylized Phantoms, in the larger context of Computational Phantoms.

Computational phantoms are mathematical models representing human or animal anatomy used to calculate internal radiation dosimetry
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}. By representing the body's organs and structures, these phantoms allow computational software (such as OLINDA/EXM) to estimate the mean absorbed radiation dose to specific targets using the Medical Internal Radiation Dose (MIRD) formalism and pre-calculated S-values
 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}.
Within this broader context, anthropomorphic stylized phantoms—such as the widely known Cristy-Eckerman series and Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) models—represent the earliest generation of these computational tools. They define organs using simple, geometric volumes (like cylinders, spheres, and ellipsoids) to represent a "standardized" individual
 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}

 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}.
The sources highlight several critical characteristics and limitations of these stylized phantoms:
Lack of Individualization: Because they rely on fixed geometric shapes, stylized phantoms fail to capture patient-specific characteristics such as exact organ size, sex, age, weight, and unique anatomical variations
 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}.
Homogeneous Assumptions: They operate on the assumption that tissues have uniform density and that radioactivity is homogeneously distributed throughout the entire organ, which rarely reflects real biological or clinical scenarios
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}

 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}.
Poor Tumor Modeling: Stylized phantoms typically do not include specific tumor models
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}. Instead, when calculating tumor doses, lesions are often approximated as isolated unit-density tissue spheres
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. This simplification fails to account for the complex "cross-dose" radiation contributions that a tumor might deliver to, or receive from, surrounding healthy tissues
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}.
To address some of the morphological inaccuracies of a one-size-fits-all model, researchers have developed population-specific stylized phantoms. For example, stylized phantoms were created specifically for the Japanese reference male and the Chinese mathematical phantom (CMP) using Asian reference data
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}. These population-tailored models yielded simulated doses that much more closely matched actual thermoluminescent dosimeter measurements compared to the generic Cristy-Eckerman phantoms
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}.
The Evolution to NURBS and Voxel-Based Phantoms To overcome the rigid nature of stylized geometries, computational phantoms have evolved into hybrid models based on Non-Uniform Rational B-Spline (NURBS) technology, such as the NURBS-based cardiac-torso (NCAT) phantom and the RADAR reference adult phantoms
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}

 \cite{GuptaLee2020}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. These advanced hybrid phantoms bridge the gap between realistic patient anatomy and the mathematical flexibility of stylized phantoms
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}. Because their surfaces can be easily deformed, NURBS phantoms can be customized, scaled, or morphed to better match an individual patient's body contour and organ masses
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}

 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}
 \cite{KostDewaraja2015}. Upgrading from stylized phantoms to NURBS-based phantoms (for instance, moving from OLINDA/EXM v. 1.0 to v. 2.0) produces more accurate dosimetry because it offers a better anatomical representation where tissues correctly contact one another
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}.
Ultimately, the field of internal dosimetry is pivoting away from standardized phantoms entirely, shifting toward patient-specific, voxel-based models
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{GuptaLee2020}. Voxel-based dosimetry utilizes actual tomographic imaging (like CT or MRI) to create a precise 3D digital model of the individual patient or animal
 \cite{GuptaLee2020}. The sources note that relying strictly on stylized, generalized phantoms can lead to significant discrepancies. For example, in preclinical mouse models, it was found that calculated S-values could deviate by as much as 80\\\\% from their true value if the specific shape, mass, and relative location of organs were not accurately modeled using voxel-level data
 \cite{GuptaLee2020}.

Traditional internal dosimetry, such as the organ-level Medical Internal Radiation Dose (MIRD) methodology, relies heavily on standardized reference computational phantoms
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}. A major limitation of these standard phantoms is that they assume the patient's geometry and organ volumes perfectly match a generic mathematical model, and they calculate doses based on the assumption that radiopharmaceutical activity is distributed homogeneously throughout entire organs
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}. Because these standard phantoms cannot account for individual variations in body size, shape, tissue composition, or heterogeneous radiotracer uptake, they often lead to inaccurate dose estimations, particularly for complex anatomical structures and tumors
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}.
To overcome the limitations of generic computational phantoms, there is a growing shift toward voxelized patient-specific models, which represent the patient's true anatomy
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}. Instead of relying on a generalized reference model, these approaches utilize 3D imaging data (such as SPECT/CT or PET/CT) to segment the patient's actual body into a 3D matrix of tiny volumetric elements, or "voxels"
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}. The computed tomography (CT) images provide a detailed, voxel-by-voxel map of the patient's specific tissue densities (e.g., differentiating between bone, lung, and soft tissue), while the functional SPECT or PET images provide a highly accurate, non-uniform map of the radioactive decays (time-integrated activity) occurring within those specific tissues
 \cite{BashirIqbal2024}

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}.
Within these voxelized patient-specific geometries, the radiation transport and absorbed dose can be calculated using specialized techniques:

Discuss what these sources say about Cycle-Dependent Uptake Decline, in the larger context of Pharmacokinetic (PK) Modelling.

In the context of pharmacokinetic (PK) modelling for radiopharmaceutical therapy (RPT), cycle-dependent uptake decline refers to the phenomenon where the accumulation of a radioligand within target tissues steadily decreases during subsequent treatment cycles. PK models, which mathematically describe the physiological mechanisms of drug distribution, binding, and elimination, are crucial for tracking these inter-cycle variations (often termed inter-occasion variability or IOV)
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Evidence of Tumour Uptake Decline Clinical data and advanced population PK modelling reveal a clear, gradual decline in tumour uptake over successive therapy cycles. For instance, a PK model analysing 177Lu-PSMA-I&T therapy demonstrated that the tumour uptake rate significantly decreased to 73\\\\%, 50\\\\%, and 44\\\\% in cycles 2, 3, and 4–7, respectively, when compared to the initial cycle
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. Correspondingly, another study observed that the average absorbed dose in tumour lesions dropped from 3.5 Gy/GBq in the first cycle to 3.3, 2.7, and 2.4 Gy/GBq in the second, third, and fourth cycles, respectively
 \cite{OkamotoThieme2016}
 \cite{OkamotoThieme2016}.
Physiological Mechanisms PK models suggest several underlying biological and physiological mechanisms for this cycle-dependent decline:
Tumour Cell Kill and Receptor Depletion: Tumours effectively act as "antigen sinks"
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}. As the radiation from earlier cycles successfully kills cancer cells, the overall tumour volume is reduced, leading to a corresponding drop in disease markers (such as PSA) and a decreased presence of target receptors (like PSMA)
 \cite{OkamotoThieme2016}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. Consequently, there are fewer specific binding sites available for the radioligand in later cycles, directly altering the body's biodistribution
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}.
Vascular Damage: The cytotoxic radiation damage inflicted on the tumour microenvironment during initial cycles may compromise tumour vascularisation, impairing the delivery and perfusion of the radiopharmaceutical in subsequent treatments
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Impact on Normal Organs This cycle-dependent decline also impacts the biodistribution of the radiotracer in healthy organs-at-risk:
The Tumour Sink Effect: In theory, as the tumour mass shrinks and absorbs less of the radioligand, the excess unbound tracer could increasingly accumulate in normal tissues or be excreted via the kidneys, potentially raising the absorbed dose to these organs in later cycles
 \cite{BrattDrevin2016}. However, some analyses indicate that the absorbed dose in normal organs can remain similar or even decrease in the second cycle, suggesting that the "tumour sink effect" does not uniformly result in higher organ toxicity in later cycles
 \cite{PetersPriv2021}.
Tissue Recovery Rates: A PK study on salivary gland uptake noted a slight decline to 84.6\\\\% during the second cycle, indicating that normal tissues may not fully recover from radiation-induced damage within a short (e.g., two-week) interval
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. Interestingly, by the third cycle (approximately twelve weeks later), uptake rebounded to 98.1\\\\%, suggesting that the cells had eventually restored themselves or compensated through increased expression or perfusion
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Implications for PK Modelling and Treatment Planning The existence of significant cycle-dependent uptake decline highlights a major limitation in assuming constant pharmacokinetics across an entire treatment regimen. Because disease progression and prior treatments continuously alter biodistribution, relying on a single set of measurements from the first cycle to plan multiple successive cycles is generally inadequate
 \cite{KlettingSchimmel2015}.
The gradual decrease in tumour accumulation, coupled with varying recovery rates in normal tissues, strongly advocates for dynamic, personalised dosing schedules over fixed-activity protocols
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. For example, PK models suggest it may be more clinically effective to administer higher activities of the radiopharmaceutical during the initial cycles when tumour uptake is maximal, and then systematically reduce the dose in later cycles to protect organs-at-risk as tumour targeting declines
 \cite{BrattDrevin2016}

 \cite{BrattDrevin2016}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.
Discuss what these sources say about Density-Weighted Region Average, in the larger context of Averaging Strategies.

In voxel-based dosimetry, patient-specific 3D absorbed dose distributions often need to be aggregated into scalar averages across entire organs, paired organs, or multiple tumor lesions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Within these averaging strategies, two primary definitions are used for aggregating doses within a single spatial region: the simple region-average (unweighted) and the density-weighted region-average
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
The density-weighted region-average calculates the mean absorbed dose by weighting the point-wise absorbed doses by the spatially varying mass density of the tissue
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Mathematically, this equals the total absorbed energy in the region divided by the region's total mass
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. In contrast, a simple region-average integrates the absorbed dose unweighted over the spatial volume
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
The sources highlight several critical points about the density-weighted region-average in the larger context of dosimetry calculations:
Impact of Tissue Heterogeneity: The simple region-average and the density-weighted region-average only coincide if the tissue density is perfectly uniform throughout the volume of interest
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. However, in regions with markedly non-uniform density distributions—such as soft tissue mixed with lung or bone—the choice of averaging strategy leads to substantially different absorbed-dose estimates
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Stability and Consistency: The density-weighted region-average is highly stable and relatively insensitive to variations in activity and density distributions, whereas the unweighted region-average fluctuates depending on how activity is distributed
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Physical and Formal Validity: From a physics perspective, the density-weighted region-average is the most meaningful measure because ionizing radiation interacts with the actual matter in a region rather than just the spatial volume it occupies
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Furthermore, it is the only averaging strategy within a region that is strictly consistent with the ICRU's formal definition of mean absorbed dose and the standard MIRD formalism
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}

 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Graphical Representation (DMHs): When characterizing the non-uniformity of absorbed doses within a region, the density-weighted approach corresponds to generating Dose-Mass Histograms (DMHs)
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. This is viewed as a physically more meaningful representation of dose non-uniformity than traditional Dose-Volume Histograms (DVHs), which correspond to unweighted region-averages
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}

 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Coherence Across Multiple Regions: When aggregating doses between multiple separated regions (like multiple metastatic lesions), it is logically coherent to combine the density-weighted region-averages of individual lesions using a mass-weighted average across all lesions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Mixing definitions, such as combining unweighted region-averages within a region with a mass-weighted average across multiple regions, is considered mathematically illogical
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Ultimately, because these strategies can yield drastically different results, specifying that a density-weighted region-average was used is crucial for accurately reporting radionuclide therapy dosimetry, establishing dose-effect relationships, and ensuring valid comparisons between different calculation methods
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Discuss what these sources say about Grid-based Boltzmann Solver (GBBS), in the larger context of Voxel-Based Dosimetry.

Voxel-based dosimetry seeks to overcome the limitations of traditional organ-averaged models by utilizing patient-specific imaging (SPECT/CT or PET/CT) to map non-uniform radiopharmaceutical distribution and heterogeneous tissue densities
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}. While direct Monte Carlo (MC) radiation transport is considered the gold standard for these calculations due to its high accuracy in heterogeneous media, it requires millions of simulated particle histories and is often too computationally expensive for routine clinical use
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}. Conversely, fast methods like dose voxel kernel (DVK) convolution struggle to accurately account for complex tissue interfaces, even with density scaling
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}.
Grid-based Boltzmann Solvers (GBBS)—also referred to as deterministic Linear Boltzmann Transport Equation (LBTE) solvers—have emerged as an advanced solution to bridge this gap, offering MC-level accuracy at speeds comparable to convolution methods
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}

 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}.
Rather than simulating random individual particle tracks stochastically like MC methods, a GBBS solves the coupled transport equations for electrons and photons deterministically by discretizing phase space variables: space, angle, and energy
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}

 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}. Because they do not rely on random sampling, deterministic solvers completely avoid the statistical noise inherent in MC simulations
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}. Theoretically, both MC and deterministic solvers converge to the exact same physical solution in the limit of infinite histories (for MC) or infinite discretization (for GBBS), provided they use the same energy cross-sections
 \cite{BaileyMarquis2022}.
The sources highlight two prominent implementations of this technology in the context of nuclear medicine:
ATTILA (A general-purpose GBBS) Originally utilized for external megavoltage photon beams and high-energy brachytherapy, ATTILA has been evaluated for the much lower energy regimes typical of unsealed nuclear medicine sources
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}. ATTILA utilizes an unstructured tetrahedral mesh and solves for energy- and angular-dependent particle flux
 \cite{MikellKappadath2016}.

In the context of multi-region dosimetry calculation, a volume-weighted average is a specific strategy used to aggregate absorbed doses across anatomically separated areas, such as multiple tumor lesions or paired organs like the kidneys. When calculating the mean dose across a union of several distinct regions, the overall region-average is mathematically equivalent to the volume-weighted average of the individual region-averages
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
This strategy fundamentally contrasts with an unweighted average, which treats every region equally regardless of its physical size
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. The sources illustrate that volume-weighted averages are critical for accurately representing asymmetric disease burdens
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. For example, if a patient has multiple tumors where a single massive lesion accounts for 70\\\\% of the total tumor volume, an unweighted average would give a tiny, mathematically insignificant lesion the exact same influence as the massive one
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. This can drastically skew results, potentially reducing the reported average dose rate by over 50\\\\% (e.g., dropping a reported value from 147 mGy/h down to 71 mGy/h in one clinical case)
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. By weighting the average according to each region's specific volume, the massive tumor appropriately dictates the final calculation, providing a much more realistic reflection of the energy deposited in the overall tumor burden
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Furthermore, the volume-weighted average corresponds directly to the generation of combined Dose-Volume Histograms (DVHs) across multiple sub-regions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. When comparing it to other averaging strategies, clinical studies involving therapies like 177Lu-PSMA and 177Lu-DOTA-TATE show that volume-weighted averages yield very similar numerical results to mass-weighted averages
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Both strategies similarly track the physical extent of the tissue being irradiated, unlike unweighted histograms (DUHs) which only count the number of regions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
While volume-weighted averages are highly practical and correspond directly to standard region-average calculations, the sources caution against mixing incompatible averaging frameworks. For example, it is logically coherent to pair a simple region-average within an organ with a volume-weighted average between organs
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Conversely, pairing a simple region-average within an organ with a mass-weighted average between organs is considered logically inconsistent
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Ultimately, because volume-weighted, mass-weighted, and unweighted averages can produce substantially different dosimetric estimates depending on patient anatomy and tumor distribution, it is imperative that clinicians and researchers explicitly state which averaging strategy they employ to ensure valid method comparisons and safe clinical evaluations
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.

Region-average: A simple, unweighted spatial average across the region's volume, corresponding to traditional Dose-Volume Histograms (DVHs)

Density-weighted region-average: This strategy weights the point-wise absorbed doses by the mass density of the tissue, effectively dividing the absorbed energy by the mass of the region

 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}

 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Mass-Weighted Average Between Regions When calculating a single mean absorbed dose across multiple separated regions, three methods are commonly defined: the volume-weighted average, the unweighted average, and the mass-weighted average

The mass-weighted average computes the overall dose by combining the total absorbed energy of all the individual sub-regions and dividing it by their total combined mass
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. The sources highlight several key insights regarding its use:
Logical Coherence: In practice, averaging strategies are often combined. The sources argue that using a density-weighted average within individual regions is logically coherent when paired with a mass-weighted average between those regions
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. Conversely, pairing a simple spatial region-average with a mass-weighted average across regions is considered illogical
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Physical Meaningfulness: Because ionizing radiation interacts with physical matter rather than just the spatial volume it occupies, the sources conclude that from a physics perspective, the density-weighted region-average and the mass-weighted average between regions are the most physically meaningful measures of absorbed dose
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Impact on Results vs. Unweighted Averages: When calculating the average dose across multiple tumors, mass-weighted and volume-weighted averages generally yield similar results, but they can differ substantially from unweighted averages
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. For example, if a patient has multiple lesions where one massive tumor accounts for 70\\\\% of the total tumor burden, an unweighted average gives small lesions the exact same mathematical influence as the massive one
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}. This can severely skew the reported data, sometimes resulting in an unweighted average absorbed-dose rate that is up to 50\\\\% lower than what the density- and mass-weighted averages report
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.
Mass Scaling for Organ S-Values In a related context of mass-based adjustments, when utilizing traditional Organ S-Values (OSVs) rather than voxel-level dosimetry, mass scaling is considered essential
 \cite{BroschLenzKurkowska2024}. Mass scaling adapts standard-phantom S-values to patient-specific organ masses to yield accurate dose values
 \cite{BroschLenzKurkowska2024}. If volumes of interest are generated at multiple time points, the sources recommend using the average mass of the organ across those time points for the scaling process
 \cite{BroschLenzKurkowska2024}.
Ultimately, because different strategies can lead to drastically different outcomes, the sources strongly recommend that clinicians and researchers explicitly state which averaging strategy (e.g., mass-weighted vs. unweighted) they are using when reporting dosimetry to prevent misleading comparisons
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}.

In the context of targeted radioligand therapies such as 177Lu-PSMA and 177Lu-DOTATATE, the bone marrow is recognised as a critical dose-limiting organ at risk (OAR)
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. Systemic administration of these radiopharmaceuticals exposes the blood elements and haematopoietic stem cells to radiation, which can lead to severe, albeit often transient, haematological toxicities such as thrombocytopenia, neutropenia, and lymphopenia

 \cite{BlakkisrudPeterson2024}

 \cite{GrobPriv2024}.

 \cite{BlakkisrudPeterson2024}
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. However, the sources advise caution when strictly adhering to this 2 Gy limit for modern radioligand therapies
 \cite{BlakkisrudPeterson2024}. This historical threshold was originally derived using blood as a surrogate organ for red marrow during the treatment of differentiated thyroid cancer with 131I-NaI
 \cite{BlakkisrudPeterson2024}.  \cite{BlakkisrudPeterson2024}
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}.

 \cite{NautiyalJha2022}
 \cite{DelkerFendler2015}. Median absorbed doses are frequently reported in the range of 0.012 to 0.044 Gy/GBq
 \cite{DelkerFendler2015}

 \cite{PetersPriv2021}
 \cite{SarnelliBelli2019}. Therefore, even when accumulating the dose over five or six standard therapy cycles (typically 6 to 7.4 GBq each), the total absorbed dose to the bone marrow usually remains well below 2 Gy

 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}
 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}. The sources highlight a significant discrepancy between the two primary measurement techniques:
Blood-Based Dosimetry: Historically, this method has been the gold standard. It assumes that the radiopharmaceutical does not specifically bind to red marrow cells, meaning the activity concentration in the marrow is assumed to be directly proportional or equal to the activity measured in the blood
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}

 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}
 \cite{GrobPriv2024}.

 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}
 \cite{GrobPriv2024}.

 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}
 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}. The higher doses detected by imaging suggest there may be specific binding of 177Lu-PSMA to bone marrow cells (or somatostatin-receptor expression on haematopoietic stem cells in the case of 177Lu-DOTATATE) that the blood-based surrogate method fails to capture
 \cite{VallotBrillouet2024}
 \cite{GrobPriv2024}.

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. In these instances, traditional blood-based dosimetry models severely underestimate the absolute dose delivered to the marrow space
 \cite{NautiyalJha2022}
 \cite{KabasakalAbuQbeitah2015}.

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}
 \cite{NautiyalJha2022}. This heavily pre-treated status significantly increases their risk of developing severe haematotoxicity, even when the radiation dose to the bone marrow remains strictly below the 2 Gy limit

In targeted radiopharmaceutical therapy (TRT), the patient's anatomy is broadly categorized into two domains: target organs (the tumour lesions) and risk organs (healthy organs at risk, or OARs, such as the kidneys, salivary glands, liver, and bone marrow)
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}. The fundamental objective of TRT is to deliver a maximum, highly localized radiation-absorbed dose to the target tumour lesions in order to induce cell death, while simultaneously restricting the absorbed dose in the risk organs below established toxicity thresholds

 \cite{PetersHofferber2021}
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}
 \cite{zkanUar2020}.

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}
 \cite{SarnelliBelli2019}
 \cite{zkanUar2020}. Consequently, tumour lesions routinely receive absorbed doses that are significantly higher—often 3 to 6 times higher—than the doses delivered to critical organs at risk
 \cite{ChuamsaamarkkeeCharoenphun2025}

 \cite{NautiyalJha2022}
 \cite{NautiyalJha2022}. This favorable therapeutic index ensures that the radiation is effectively targeting the disease while sparing healthy tissues
 \cite{ChuamsaamarkkeeCharoenphun2025}

 \cite{DelkerFendler2015}
 \cite{DelkerFendler2015}.

 \cite{FinocchiaroBerenato2020}
 \cite{HippelinenTenhunen2017}
 \cite{DuongSarno2025}
 \cite{GoetzLang2020}. The sources highlight several critical factors influencing the dosimetric profile of tumour lesions in relation to the wider organ context:

 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}.

 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}
 \cite{PetersPriv2021}.  \cite{PetersPriv2021}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}.

In the treatment monitoring of metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) undergoing 177Lu-PSMA radioligand therapy, prostate-specific antigen (PSA) dynamics serve as a primary clinical marker for evaluating therapeutic response and disease progression
 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}. A positive treatment response is typically defined as a PSA decrease of 50\\\\% or more, whereas disease progression is marked by two consecutive increases in PSA levels totalling at least 25\\\\% above the nadir
 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}

 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}
 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}. Despite the general efficacy of the therapy, achieving a significant PSA response is not guaranteed; for instance, the phase 3 VISION trial demonstrated that only 46\\\\% of patients achieved a PSA response
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}.

 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}. Clinical studies demonstrate a moderate correlation between the mean lesion AD and the percentage change in PSA after the first treatment cycle
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}. Specifically, patients receiving a higher mean AD (greater than 7.5 Gy) exhibit a significantly better PSA response—with a median PSA drop of 70\\\\% compared to a 25\\\\% increase in patients receiving less than 7.5 Gy
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}. This improved PSA response directly translates to a longer biochemical progression-free survival (bPFS)
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}. Furthermore, pre-therapeutic PSA doubling time moderately correlates with PSA response, and combining PSA doubling time with AD estimates provides a stronger overall predictor for biochemical response
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}.

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. The direct effect of the radiation on PSA levels is often limited for the majority of patients; instead, the delayed effect significantly drives the overall treatment response
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. This delayed decline in PSA can continue even after the treatment is completed and the radioactivity has been eliminated from the tumours
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.

Tumour Microenvironment & Bystander Effects: The delay may be caused by radiation-induced changes to the tumour microenvironment (such as alterations in vascularisation) or a radiation-induced bystander response, where non-irradiated cells show damage resulting from interactions with the immune system

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}.

 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. For example, simulations comparing a standard '4 x 6' regimen (four cycles with a six-week interval) to a '2 x 2' regimen (two cycles with a two-week interval, repeated after twelve weeks) showed similar overall stable disease response rates
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. However, the '2 x 2' scheme induced a more prominent decrease in PSA immediately following the second cycle
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}
 \cite{SiebingaVeen2024}. Ultimately, modelling individual PSA dynamics helps clinicians identify population response rates, predict individual treatment outcomes, and identify patients in whom radioligand therapy is likely to fail so that treatments can be personalised

The theranostic pairing of 68Ga and 177Lu—particularly when utilizing prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) ligands like PSMA-11, PSMA-617, and PSMA-I&T—is a foundational concept in personalized radiopharmaceutical therapy (RPT) for advanced prostate cancer.  \cite{DuongSarno2025}
 \cite{DuongSarno2025}.

 \cite{KennedyLugassi2020}

 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}
 \cite{GrkovskiKrebs2025}. Beyond merely qualifying a patient for treatment, the diagnostic scan serves as a powerful predictive monitoring tool.  \cite{OkamotoThieme2016}

 \cite{WangZang2019}
 \cite{WangZang2019}. Consequently, a high pre-therapeutic PET SUV can serve as a simple, reliable surrogate indicator for a high therapeutic absorbed dose

 \cite{OkamotoThieme2016}
 \cite{XueGafita2022}.  \cite{XueGafita2022}
 \cite{PetersHofferber2021}.

 \cite{PetersHofferber2021}. By utilizing this indirect theranostic approach, clinicians can shift away from a rigid "one-size-fits-all" fixed activity regimen toward highly personalized, dosimetry-driven dosing

 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}.

 \cite{XueGafita2022}. Furthermore, therapeutic tracers are often administered at much higher activities than imaging tracers, and the specific peptides used (e.g., diagnostic 68Ga-PSMA-11 versus therapeutic 177Lu-PSMA-617) may not be perfectly identical, which can introduce variations in intratumoral residence times
 \cite{XueGafita2022}
 \cite{WangZang2019}.

 \cite{XueGafita2022}
 \cite{XueGafita2022}. These models learn quantitative principles and multi-organ relationships from training data to more accurately forecast 177Lu-PSMA absorbed doses, reducing the likelihood of underestimating or overestimating the true therapeutic biodistribution

By replacing traditional 2D planar imaging and generic, stylized phantoms, hybrid SPECT/CT provides a highly accurate, patient-specific 3D map of both anatomical tissue density (via CT) and the spatiotemporal distribution of the radiotracer (via SPECT)

 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}
 \cite{DewarajaMirando2022}.

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. Furthermore, quantitative accuracy depends heavily on iterative reconstruction algorithms (such as OSEM) that incorporate physical corrections for photon attenuation (using the CT map), scatter, and collimator-detector response (resolution recovery)

what are physical, biological, and effective half-lives in Lutetium PSMA

In the context of targeted radioligand therapy using Lutetium-177 (177Lu) PSMA, the physical, biological, and effective half-lives describe the different rates at which the radioactive drug diminishes within the patient's body.
Physical Half-Life The physical half-life is a fixed property of the radioisotope itself, representing the time it takes for half of the radioactive atoms to undergo radioactive decay, completely independent of biological processes. For 177Lu, the physical half-life is approximately 6.64 to 6.7 days (roughly 160 hours)
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{EllsGrogan2024}
 \cite{KennedyLugassi2020}
 \cite{PistoneAuditore2022}. This is considered highly convenient for clinical use, as it is long enough to manage delivery and allow the radioligand to accumulate in tumors, but short enough to spare healthy organs from excessive, prolonged radiation
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}.
Biological Half-Life The biological half-life refers to the time it takes for the patient's body to naturally clear or excrete half of the administered pharmaceutical compound, assuming the substance is not radioactive. Because 177Lu-PSMA has complex pharmacokinetics, different organs eliminate the drug at different rates. For example, pharmacokinetic modeling of the kidneys shows that the biological clearance of 177Lu-PSMA often features a fast initial washout phase with a biological half-life of around 11.5 hours, followed by a slower secondary elimination phase with a biological half-life of roughly 76.4 hours
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}. Another simulation study noted a median biological half-life in the kidneys of 50 hours starting from 24 hours post-injection
 \cite{RinscheidKletting2019}.
Effective Half-Life The effective half-life is the most critical metric for clinical dosimetry. It represents the actual, observed rate at which radioactivity declines in a specific organ or tumor
 \cite{StraubKupferschlger2023}. It is the mathematical combination of both the physical decay of the 177Lu and the biological clearance of the PSMA molecule. Because these two depletion processes happen simultaneously, the effective half-life is always shorter than both the physical and biological half-lives.
Because the radioligand targets and binds to specific tissues differently, the effective half-life varies significantly between normal organs at risk and the targeted tumor lesions:
Organs at Risk: Normal organs typically exhibit faster radiotracer washout. Clinical studies report median effective half-lives of roughly 28 to 39 hours for the kidneys
 \cite{PetersHofferber2021}

 \cite{PetersPriv2021}
 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}, 32.5 to 42 hours for the salivary glands (parotid and submandibular)
 \cite{PetersPriv2021}

 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}
 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}, and 19 to 25 hours for the liver
 \cite{PetersPriv2021}
 \cite{SarnelliBelli2019}.
Tumor Lesions: Because the therapy is designed to bind strongly to the PSMA receptors overexpressed on prostate cancer cells, tumors demonstrate much longer tracer retention. The effective half-life in tumor lesions (both bone and soft tissue) is generally much longer, typically ranging from 50 to 72 hours
 \cite{PetersHofferber2021}

 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}
 \cite{KarimzadehSchatz2024}. This prolonged effective half-life in tumors ensures that a higher, lethal radiation dose is delivered to the cancer cells while the radioactivity clears more rapidly from healthy tissues.

Time-Integrated Activity (TIA), often referred to as cumulated activity or residence time, represents the total number of radioactive decays (nuclear disintegrations) that occur within a specific source region—such as a tumor lesion, an organ, or a microscopic voxel—over a defined dose-integration period
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. In targeted radiopharmaceutical therapy, this period is typically extrapolated to infinity to account for the entire lifespan of the drug in the body
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}.
Mathematically, TIA is defined as the area under the time-activity curve (TAC)
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}. The TAC tracks the patient-specific bio-kinetics of 177Lu-PSMA, capturing how rapidly the radioligand is absorbed into tissues, how long it is retained, and how quickly it is biologically excreted and physically decays
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}
 \cite{RamonahengQebetu2024}.
Role in Dosimetry Within the standard Medical Internal Radiation Dose (MIRD) formalism and voxel-based dosimetry, TIA serves as the foundational "source" component for calculating the absorbed radiation dose
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}. To determine the total absorbed dose delivered to a target region, the estimated TIA is multiplied by a corresponding S-value or Dose Kernel
 \cite{GtzLang2021}

 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}
 \cite{GustafssonLjungberg2023}
 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}. While the S-value accounts for the physical transport of energy through the patient's anatomy, the TIA provides the absolute measure of how much radioactive energy was released at the source
 \cite{FuertesGarca2021}

 \cite{ChatachotShiratori2021}
 \cite{HardiansyahYousefzadehNowshahr2024}.
Calculation Methods for 177Lu-PSMA Because measuring continuous radioactivity in a patient is impossible, clinicians utilize various methods to sample the activity and estimate the TIA:
Multiple-Time-Point (MTP) Imaging: The gold standard approach involves acquiring sequential SPECT/CT or planar scans over several days (e.g., at 4, 24, 48, 72, and 168 hours post-injection)
 \cite{SiebingaPriv2023}
 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}. The activity measured at these discrete points is used to plot the TAC.  \cite{DewarajaMirando2022}
 \cite{BroschLenzDelker2023}. These methods rely on established population-based effective half-lives or specific mathematical formulas (such as the Hänscheid or Madsen methods) to extrapolate the full TAC from a single data point

 \cite{DewarajaMirando2022}
 \cite{ChenLu2023}.

 \cite{DuongSarno2025}
 \cite{DuongSarno2025}.
Clinical Challenges The accuracy of the TIA calculation is heavily dependent on the chosen imaging schedule

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}

what is Dose Voxel Kernel for lu psma

A Dose Voxel Kernel (DVK), also commonly referred to as a Voxel S-Value (VSV), is a 3D mathematical matrix that represents the mean absorbed radiation dose delivered to a target voxel per nuclear disintegration (unit activity) that occurs in a source voxel
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}

 \cite{GleisnerChouin2022}
 \cite{StabinMadsen2019}. It is essentially a discretized version of a continuous Dose Point Kernel (DPK) mapped onto a specific 3D grid
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}

 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}.
In the context of 177Lu-PSMA targeted radioligand therapy, DVKs act as the computational engine for patient-specific dosimetry. They are used to transform a patient's Time-Integrated Activity (TIA) map—the spatial distribution of radioactive decays derived from sequential SPECT/CT images—into a highly detailed 3D absorbed dose distribution
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}

 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}. This is achieved by convolving the TIA map with the DVK, an operation typically executed rapidly using Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) or Fast Hartley Transform algorithms
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2012}

 \cite{DewarajaMirando2022}
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}.
Characteristics of 177Lu Kernels Because 177Lu decays by emitting both beta particles (electrons) and gamma photons, a 177Lu DVK must account for the distinct energy deposition patterns of both radiation types
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}:
Beta Electrons: These particles have a very short average range in tissue (less than 3 mm, with a maximum range of approximately 1.7 mm in soft tissue)
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{PistoneAuditore2022}. Consequently, the vast majority of the radiation energy (roughly 70\\\\% or more) is deposited directly in the central source voxel where the decay occurred
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}.
Gamma Photons: 177Lu also emits photons (primarily at 113 keV and 208 keV) which have a much longer, effectively infinite range
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}. While their overall dose contribution is much smaller than the beta emissions, they are responsible for the "cross-dose" delivered to surrounding peripheral voxels
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{TranGiaSalasRamirez2019}.
Generation and Resampling DVKs are inherently specific to a given radioisotope, voxel size, and tissue composition
 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}.
They are initially generated using highly computationally intensive Monte Carlo (MC) radiation transport simulations (such as GEANT4, MCNP, or EGSnrc) simulating millions of particle histories within a standardized medium, most commonly water or soft tissue

 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}
 \cite{GtzLang2021}
 \cite{GtzSchmidkonz2019}.
Because clinical SPECT and PET images are acquired at varying spatial resolutions (e.g., 3 mm to 6 mm voxel dimensions), base DVKs are usually simulated at very fine spatial resolutions (e.g., 0.5 mm for electrons and 1.0 mm for photons)
 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}

 \cite{DieudonnHobbs2010}
 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}
 \cite{FernndezHnscheid2013}. These high-resolution kernels are then analytically resampled, interpolated, and scaled to perfectly match the arbitrary voxel dimensions of a specific patient's clinical imaging data
