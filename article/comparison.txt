The Evolution of Differentiable Medical Image Processing in Julia: A Comparative Analysis of MedImages.jl and Legacy Ecosystem Tooling
Introduction to the Paradigm of Medical Image Processing
The landscape of biomedical imaging is currently undergoing a structural transformation, driven by the rapid integration of artificial intelligence (AI), high-throughput radiomics, and precision theranostics. As hybrid functional and anatomical modalities—most notably Positron Emission Tomography/Computed Tomography (PET/CT) and Single-Photon Emission Computed Tomography/Computed Tomography (SPECT/CT)—cement their roles as the definitive gold standards for oncologic staging and targeted radionuclide therapy, the sheer volume and complexity of the resulting data have expanded exponentially. Modern clinical research, particularly multi-center trials and biobank-scale machine learning initiatives, rely heavily on massive three-dimensional (3D) and four-dimensional (4D) datasets. To derive mathematically robust and clinically valid insights from these heterogeneous modalities, the underlying voxel data must be systematically resampled, perfectly aligned, and quantitatively normalized without suffering any loss of spatial or clinical metadata.   

Historically, the computational burden of executing these geometric transformations has been managed by monolithic C++ frameworks, such as the Insight Toolkit (ITK), and their corresponding Python wrappers, such as SimpleITK and MONAI. While these legacy ecosystems provide a broad repertoire of established image processing filters and registration algorithms, the emergence of Scientific Machine Learning (SciML) has exposed their fundamental architectural limitations. SciML relies on end-to-end differentiable programming, intertwining classical physics-based numerical solvers with deep neural networks. In a typical SciML workflow, every discrete mathematical operation—including spatial preprocessing, geometric augmentation, and anatomical resampling—must remain differentiable to allow gradients to flow backward through the computational graph. When these spatial operations are dispatched to compiled, non-differentiable C++ backends, the chain of automatic differentiation is immediately severed, preventing the optimization of upstream geometric parameters and physically-informed variables.   

The Julia programming language has emerged as the premier environment for addressing the rigorous demands of SciML. Designed from its inception to solve the two-language problem, Julia combines high-level syntax with Just-In-Time (JIT) compilation to deliver execution speeds comparable to C and Fortran. More importantly, its multiple dispatch architecture and native compiler-level automatic differentiation engines enable gradients to propagate effortlessly through native operations. Despite this immense theoretical advantage, Julia’s adoption in the medical imaging community has been historically stifled by a fragmented, unmaintained, and functionally incomplete package ecosystem. Foundational tools like NIfTI.jl and DICOM.jl have remained strictly confined to basic file parsing, offering virtually no support for spatial transformations. Efforts to wrap established external libraries, such as ITK.jl, collapsed under the weight of cross-language friction years ago, leaving practitioners with severe developmental roadblocks.   

This comprehensive report provides an exhaustive, comparative analysis of the contemporary Julia medical imaging ecosystem. It rigorously evaluates the structural deficiencies, extended periods of unmaintained stagnation, and geometric limitations of alternative libraries such as NIfTI.jl, DICOM.jl, and ITK.jl. It then contrasts these legacy frameworks with the recently developed MedImages.jl ecosystem. By analyzing empirical benchmarks in biobank-scale scalability, quantitative metadata fidelity, and algorithmic differentiability, this report demonstrates how MedImages.jl systematically resolves the historical limitations of the Julia imaging landscape and establishes a new standard for computational nuclear medicine.   

The Theoretical Imperative: Metadata Integrity and Spatial Mathematics
To accurately critique the limitations of the legacy Julia medical imaging libraries, one must first define the strict mathematical and physical constraints governing multimodal spatial analysis. Unlike standard two-dimensional computer vision formats (e.g., JPEG, PNG), medical images are absolute physical measurements bounded to a specific anatomical space and time. Treating them merely as unanchored multi-dimensional arrays fundamentally destroys their diagnostic and quantitative utility.

A Critical Appraisal of Legacy Julia Medical Imaging Libraries
An exhaustive review of the open-source Julia landscape prior to the architectural inception of MedImages.jl reveals a deeply scattered ecosystem populated by single-purpose, incomplete, or entirely abandoned packages. The over-reliance on these tools has historically forced researchers to write and maintain vast amounts of redundant "glue code," resulting in severe execution bottlenecks, high compilation latencies, and persistent metadata degradation.

NIfTI.jl: Structural Limitations of a Pure I/O Utility
The Neuroimaging Informatics Technology Initiative (NIfTI) format is an essential standard in the neuroimaging community and general biomedical research, primarily favored for its streamlined approach to encoding multi-dimensional brain data compared to the complexities of DICOM. To address the need to handle this format natively, NIfTI.jl was developed as a conduit for reading and writing .nii and .nii.gz files within Julia. The package exposes a high-level niread function that parses a file and returns an NIVolume or NiftiImage structure. This structure compartmentalizes the data into a distinct header field and an abstract array of raw voxel data.   

While NIfTI.jl is highly effective and stable for base-level binary serialization and header extraction (retrieving parameters such as voxel_size and time_step ), it is characterized by severe functional limitations that render it entirely insufficient for building end-to-end medical image preprocessing pipelines. The most glaring and critical omission is the complete absence of native spatial transformations. NIfTI.jl does not possess the algorithmic capacity to resample an image to a new isotropic spacing, rotate an image volume, crop the field of view, or align a moving image to a fixed target. If a researcher wishes to resample a functional MRI volume to align with a structural T1-weighted anatomical grid, NIfTI.jl provides no internal mechanism to execute this task.   

Consequently, users are forced to manually extract the raw, unscaled numerical array using ni.raw  and pass it to generic, non-medical mathematical packages. Because these generic packages are entirely agnostic to the NIfTI header standard, the spatial metadata is completely discarded during the transformation phase. The researcher is then tasked with manually recalculating the new spatial dimensions, re-deriving the 4×4 affine transformation matrix, and attempting to reconstruct a valid NIfTI header from scratch—a convoluted process highly susceptible to floating-point truncation errors and catastrophic axis inversions.   

Furthermore, NIfTI.jl suffers from extended periods of developmental stagnation that severely hinder its utility in modern workflows. An analysis of the repository's history reveals that the package is largely unmaintained beyond emergency compatibility patches. Its latest major tagged release, version v0.6.1, was published on December 28, 2024, yet even this update was largely confined to minor bug fixes rather than feature expansions. The repository continues to lack critical, highly requested medical imaging functionality. In 2021, an issue (Issue #55) was formally logged requesting the ability to natively reorient NIfTI volumes based on standard anatomical axes (such as mapping from RAS to LPI orientations). The ensuing discussion highlighted that while ad-hoc user scripts existed, formal incorporation into the library was delayed indefinitely due to maintainer bandwidth constraints. Therefore, executing fundamental spatial orientation flips remains a hazardous, unverified, and entirely manual endeavor for the end-user.   

DICOM.jl: Parsing Complexity Without Geometric Context
The Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM) standard remains the ubiquitous format generated by clinical imaging hardware (CT, PET, MRI, SPECT). DICOM is notoriously complex, relying on highly nested hierarchical datasets, proprietary manufacturer tags, and a vast array of Value Representations (VR). DICOM.jl provides a mature, low-level interface for parsing these complex binary streams into navigable Julia dictionaries. It utilizes robust internal lookup tables to guess implicit VRs from hexadecimal tags and successfully extracts deeply embedded patient and acquisition metadata. Users can also supply auxiliary dictionaries to override default lookups for retired or non-standard elements.   

However, DICOM.jl shares the identical fundamental limitation observed in NIfTI.jl: it terminates completely at the boundary of file I/O. It functions purely as a dictionary parser, not as a geometric image processing engine. In modern clinical environments, a diagnostic CT or PET study rarely exists as a single cohesive file. Instead, a study is distributed across a directory containing hundreds, or sometimes thousands, of individual 2D axial slice files. Reconstructing a mathematically valid, continuous 3D isotropic voxel volume from a disorganized directory of individual DICOM slices requires sophisticated spatial heuristics. The software must read the Image Position (Patient) tags, calculate the precise normal vectors from the Image Orientation (Patient) tags, sort the instances by physical location along the Z-axis, identify and account for variable slice spacing, mitigate missing frames, and construct a singular correct 3D affine matrix.   

DICOM.jl possesses zero innate capacity to execute this spatial reconstruction. Maintainers within the DICOM.jl repository have openly acknowledged this critical architectural gap, noting that there is "an enormous amount of variability and heuristics required to do slice reconstruction reliably across manufacturers and scanner versions". Because DICOM.jl lacks an underlying geometric tensor representation, users attempting to implement 3D spatial registration or multi-modal spatial alignment directly from DICOM source files are met with a systemic void. Like NIfTI.jl, DICOM.jl suffers from a stagnant maintenance lifecycle regarding new features. Its latest release, version v0.11.0, was published on June 6, 2024, and the repository remains steadfastly scoped to raw dictionary parsing with no trajectory toward spatial image operations.   

ITK.jl: The Cross-Language Interoperability Failure
Recognizing the stark lack of native Julia spatial processing tools, the scientific community naturally attempted to bridge Julia with the monolithic C++ Insight Toolkit (ITK) and its simplified counterpart, SimpleITK. SimpleITK provides an exhaustively validated, production-grade suite of registration algorithms, complex filters, and advanced resampling interpolators. The rationale was straightforward: rather than rewriting decades of validated medical imaging mathematics in native Julia, one should simply bind the C++ library.   

The primary attempt to execute this natively, encapsulated in the cj-mclaughlin/ITK.jl repository, highlights the severe friction and ultimate failure of cross-language medical imaging infrastructure. Initiated over seven years ago, the repository achieved a mere 35 commits before being effectively and permanently abandoned. The developmental notes documented in the repository reveal the stark reality of the undertaking: despite substantial effort, the package only ever managed to support "four parameterized translation registration functions, and two simple test registration functions".   

Attempting to bridge Julia's highly dynamic runtime to a heavily templated C++ library like ITK resulted in profound Application Binary Interface (ABI) issues, compiler standard mismatches, and unresolvable segmentation faults. Developers documented significant compilation errors stemming from differences in C++ standard implementations (e.g., C++11 vs. C++14 name mangling conventions), which caused critical internal ITK symbols like itk::LightObject::Delete() to vanish during the linking phase of the shared library generation. Because ITK.jl has been unmaintained for seven years, it is entirely incompatible with modern Julia versions and completely unusable for contemporary research.   

As a pragmatic fallback, researchers have frequently utilized Julia's PyCall.jl package to invoke the Python bindings of SimpleITK, NiBabel, or ANTsPy from within their Julia scripts. While functionally viable for basic preprocessing, this architecture introduces devastating penalties for Scientific Machine Learning. First, crossing the Python/Julia language boundary repeatedly to process large 3D medical arrays incurs severe memory serialization overhead and requires constant data copying. This destroys the execution speed required for biobank-scale analyses. Second, and most critically, C++ libraries wrapped in Python are completely opaque to Julia's Automatic Differentiation compilers. An image resampled or rotated via a SimpleITK PyCall cannot propagate a gradient, explicitly preventing the geometric optimization required in end-to-end physics-informed neural networks.   

The Insufficiency of General-Purpose Julia Packages
With domain-specific tools lacking, researchers have frequently attempted to shoehorn medical imaging tasks into general-purpose Julia computer vision packages such as Images.jl and CoordinateTransformations.jl. Images.jl provides a rich suite of standard 2D operations, including warping, rotating, and filtering. However, these packages are engineered for standard photography and generic N-dimensional arrays; they possess no awareness of affine transformation matrices, DICOM tag structures, or physical voxel spacing in millimeters. Applying an imrotate from Images.jl to a NIfTI volume irreparably decouples the data from its physical orientation, making subsequent multimodal alignment mathematically impossible.   

Similarly, MRIReco.jl represents an advanced Julia package within the medical sphere, but its scope is entirely constrained to a different domain. MRIReco.jl focuses on the physical reconstruction of magnetic resonance images directly from raw ISMRMRD scanner trajectories (e.g., k-space Cartesian, radial, or spiral sampling) into spatial representations. While an exceptional tool for physicists working on scanner hardware, MRIReco.jl relies on external libraries like NIfTI.jl and DICOM.jl for saving the output , offering no solutions for the post-reconstruction geometric manipulation, multimodal registration, or deep learning preprocessing required by the broader imaging community.   

The Architectural Paradigm Shift: The MedImages.jl Framework
To systematically dismantle the computational bottlenecks, metadata degradation, and differentiability roadblocks inherent in the legacy Julia ecosystem, researchers and the JuliaHealth organization developed MedImages.jl. By engineering a comprehensive framework from the ground up entirely in native Julia, MedImages.jl simultaneously resolves the I/O isolation of NIfTI/DICOM and the rigid differentiability barriers of ITK wrappers.   

The Unified MedImage Data Structure
The core architectural divergence of MedImages.jl is its foundational data structure: the MedImage type. Unlike NIVolume in NIfTI.jl, which treats the raw numerical array and the metadata header as neighboring but functionally decoupled entities, the MedImage struct enforces a strict, unbreakable mathematical binding between the multi-dimensional voxel data and its vectorized spatial metadata. This includes the physical origin coordinates, the voxel spacing matrix, and the directional orientation vectors, alongside highly specific clinical fields such as patient identity and precise acquisition timestamps.   

When a native Julia spatial function is invoked—such as rotate_mi, resample_to_spacing, translate_mi, or crop_mi—the operation acts upon the MedImage as a holistic entity. The underlying numeric tensor is geometrically modified, and simultaneously, the 4×4 affine transformation matrix is automatically and perfectly updated according to strict mathematical rules. This design paradigm guarantees the absolute prevention of metadata drift.   

For complex multimodal studies, such as theranostic PET/CT workflows where functional and anatomical imaging must perfectly align, MedImages.jl introduces the BatchedMedImage structure. This advanced container allows disparate imaging modalities, which often occupy different physical resolutions and field-of-views, to be mathematically linked. When a geometric transform is applied to a BatchedMedImage (for instance, during a deep learning data augmentation loop that applies randomized sequential rotations and spatial translations), the exact identical continuous physical warping is applied seamlessly across both the high-resolution CT and the low-resolution PET modalities. This ensures that pixel-perfect spatial alignment is rigorously maintained across all input channels for downstream multimodal convolutional neural networks.   

Optimized I/O Routing via ITKIOWrapper.jl
Recognizing that reproducing the decades of accumulated edge-case handling in DICOM and NIfTI file parsing is a wildly inefficient duplication of effort, MedImages.jl elegantly bypasses this problem by strategically leveraging ITKIOWrapper.jl for the strict, narrow purpose of reading and writing files to the disk.   

ITKIOWrapper.jl—which is actively maintained, having released version v4.0.2 in May 2025 —acts as a highly specialized, minimal bridge to the Insight Toolkit solely for data ingestion. By relying on ITKIOWrapper.jl for I/O, MedImages.jl ensures absolute compatibility with complex clinical systems, handling messy DICOM slice reconstructions accurately. Once the image is successfully loaded from the disk and converted into the native MedImage format, the C++ dependency is entirely discarded. The actual mathematical image manipulation phase is executed completely within native Julia, protecting the user from cross-language compilation errors and ensuring full compiler transparency.   

Fused GPU Acceleration and Memory Management
Where legacy tools like SimpleITK and NIfTI.jl are traditionally constrained to CPU-bound execution, MedImages.jl utilizes Julia's KernelAbstractions.jl to deploy complex spatial operations natively to parallel GPU hardware architectures. Resampling continuous 3D spatial grids is a mathematically dense operation; mapping a 512×512×512 volume requires hundreds of millions of discrete trilinear or B-spline interpolations, which typically saturates memory bandwidth and slows down data pipelines.   

MedImages.jl introduces the concept of Fused Affine transforms. These transforms mathematically collapse complex, multi-step spatial manipulation sequences (e.g., standardizing voxel spacing, applying an orientation shift, centering the physical origin, and cropping out background air) into a single, unified execution kernel. By calculating the final projection of every voxel analytically before executing a single read or write memory operation on the GPU, the framework eliminates massive intermediate memory allocations and drastically cuts memory bandwidth traffic.   

Empirical Benchmarking: Scalability and Execution Speed
The theoretical superiority of MedImages.jl over the fragmented legacy ecosystem has been empirically validated across three distinct, highly rigorous experimental axes: biobank-scale execution scalability, quantitative metadata fidelity, and algorithmic differentiability.   

High-Frequency Deep Learning Caching vs MONAI
To simulate the intense computational pressure of large-scale imaging studies, an evaluation was conducted comparing MedImages.jl against modern contemporary frameworks, notably MONAI and SimpleITK. The benchmark utilized a 100-case preprocessing and training pipeline based on the autoPET dataset, a highly realistic large-cohort scenario where multiple PET/CT studies are repeatedly loaded and harmonized during iterative deep learning model training.   

The primary preprocessing task required multimodal spatial alignment of heterogeneous PET and CT volumes onto a standardized, isotropic reference grid of 512×512×512 voxels. MedImages.jl was compared directly against MONAI's PersistentDataset caching mechanism. The integration of native High-Performance Data Format (HDF5) caching within MedImages.jl bypassed the heavy serialization costs inherent in Python-based caching systems.   

In these iterative training scenarios, MedImages.jl demonstrated paradigm-shifting processing speeds, reducing the total turnaround time per subject from approximately 650 milliseconds (under the MONAI pipeline) to just 90 milliseconds—representing a staggering 7.2x turnaround speedup.

Verification of Metadata Integrity and Quantitative Clinical Fidelity
In quantitative nuclear medicine, preserving the exact numerical mapping of radiometric intensities to physical geometry is a strict safety requirement. Unlike standard machine learning tasks, an error in spatial registration in theranostics directly results in radiation doses being improperly calculated for malignant tissue.   

Cross-Platform SUV Validation
To guarantee computational safety, a rigorous cross-platform validation was performed comparing the Standardized Uptake Value (SUV) conversion factors derived natively by MedImages.jl against the universally accepted, gold-standard SimpleITK libraries. The mathematical evaluation confirmed that the extracted SUV conversion factors matched to a precision of ≈10

This numerical equivalence ensures that researchers transitioning from legacy Python pipelines to native Julia can maintain the absolute consistency of clinical diagnostic findings while inheriting vast performance gains.   

Continuous Spatial Warping Consistency
To empirically prove that this absolute metadata integrity survives the stress of continuous spatial warping, an end-to-end transformation consistency experiment was executed across a 10-patient clinical SPECT/CT cohort. For each patient, precise automated liver segmentation masks were generated. The multimodal sequence forced the functional tracer intensities through a severe geometric pipeline: converting SPECT intensities to SUV, resampling the functional data to an anatomical CT target grid, modifying the spacing to a strict 2.0 mm isotropic grid, applying a 45-degree rotational warp around the Z-axis, and translating the physical origin by 10 voxels.   

Subsequent quantitative evaluation within the liver segmentation masks revealed a Mean SUV deviation of a mere 0.17% (ranging from 0.00% to 0.40%) and a gross volumetric liver deviation of only 0.10% (ranging from 0.01% to 0.28%). These almost negligible variances are the mathematical reality of discrete grid interpolation (i.e., expected boundary blurring effects) and definitively prove that the underlying spatial metadata matrices suffer zero drift, corruption, or degradation throughout highly complex geometric modifications.   

Enabling End-to-End Scientific Machine Learning (SciML)
The ultimate differentiating factor of MedImages.jl—and the capability that entirely separates it from NIfTI.jl, DICOM.jl, and Python-wrapped SimpleITK—is its native capacity to seamlessly interoperate with Julia's compiler-level automatic differentiation engines, namely Zygote.jl and Enzyme.jl.   

Differentiable Spatial Pipelines
When legacy Python tools execute spatial resampling via SimpleITK or ITK backends, the gradients are instantly dropped at the foreign function interface. Because core operations in MedImages.jl, such as padding, cropping, and fused affine interpolation, are written purely in native Julia syntax, reverse-mode gradients propagate analytically, continuously, and stably directly through the spatial transformation layers.   

Numerical validation tests confirm that the gradients computed on CPU and GPU architectures for fused affine interpolation maintain identical numerical trajectories within a strict tolerance of <10 
−4

Optimization of the geometric parameters resulted in a staggering reduction in reconstruction error exceeding 65% within just 20 training iterations. This definitively proves that complex spatial transformations can act as trainable layers within neural architectures, a feat impossible in isolated I/O libraries or legacy wrappers.

The Computational Burden of Monte Carlo Tracking
Calculating accurate absorbed radiation dose maps is highly complex. The gold standard methodology, Monte Carlo (MC) radiation particle tracking, provides the highest level of physical accuracy but carries an immense computational burden. Generating a high-fidelity, low-noise dose map for a single-bed SPECT study requires approximately 48 hours of continuous processing on standard clinical CPU infrastructure, creating an insurmountable bottleneck for real-time adaptive therapy workflows.   

Conversely, purely data-driven Convolutional Neural Networks (such as Dblur DoseNet) can perform inference in mere seconds. However, these architectures learn a direct image-to-dose mapping and function as opaque "black-box" predictors. Because they lack physical constraints, they are highly sensitive to out-of-distribution (OOD) shifts caused by variations in scanner hardware, crystal thickness, and institution-specific reconstruction protocols.

Conclusion
The evolution of medical image computing is moving unequivocally toward multi-modal integration and physics-informed artificial intelligence. To operate successfully in this complex paradigm, software architectures must fulfill three non-negotiable criteria. First, they must process biobank-scale volumes of data with extreme computational efficiency. Second, they must rigorously guard the physical orientation, spatial spacing, and quantitative clinical metadata that makes the imaging data diagnostically interpretable. Third, they must allow unbroken gradient propagation through geometric transformations to enable end-to-end model optimization in scientific machine learning workflows.

The legacy Julia ecosystem, heavily dependent on isolated and structurally limited I/O libraries such as NIfTI.jl and DICOM.jl, has proven entirely incapable of meeting these modern demands. These tools, largely unmaintained regarding new capabilities for over a year, lack fundamental geometric support, forcing researchers into hazardous paradigms of manual coordinate recalculation and metadata drift. The alternative route of utilizing cross-language wrappers, exemplified by the seven-year abandonment of ITK.jl, introduced insurmountable friction and entirely blocked algorithmic differentiability.

MedImages.jl systematically resolves these historical failures by unifying the mathematical representation of the multidimensional voxel grid and its physical constraints within a single, type-safe, native Julia structure. By isolating I/O routines to active dependencies like ITKIOWrapper.jl, and providing native, GPU-accelerated spatial transformations, the framework fundamentally shifts the computational bottleneck. It allows researchers to seamlessly construct advanced, differentiable SciML pipelines—such as high-fidelity Universal Differential Equation dosimetry models—reducing days of computational time down to minutes without sacrificing physical interpretability or metadata integrity. For the future of computational nuclear medicine, radiomics, and quantitative hybrid imaging within the Julia ecosystem, the native, differentiable, and metadata-aware architecture of MedImages.jl represents the definitive path forward.
