Computational Frameworks for Quantitative Lutetium-177 Dosimetry: Integrating Scientific Machine Learning with Universal Differential EquationsThe clinical implementation of targeted radionuclide therapy (TRT) has encountered a transformative era with the widespread adoption of Lutetium-177 ($^{177}Lu$) for the management of neuroendocrine tumors and metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. The therapeutic efficacy of these radiopharmaceuticals is fundamentally predicated on the delivery of a high absorbed radiation dose to malignant cells while minimizing the burden on critical organs at risk, such as the kidneys and bone marrow. However, the precision of current internal dosimetry remains significantly lower than that of external beam radiation therapy, with uncertainties often exceeding twenty percent. A primary bottleneck in achieving high-accuracy dosimetry is the qualitative nature of standard Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT) imaging, which is degraded by photon attenuation, Compton scattering, and partial volume effects. The emergence of Scientific Machine Learning (SciML) and the Universal Differential Equation (UDE) framework offers a robust pathway to transition from these qualitative representations to quantitative, physics-consistent three-dimensional dose maps. By embedding mechanistic physical laws within flexible neural architectures, it becomes possible to "undo" the degradations of the imaging system and realize Monte Carlo-quality dosimetry in clinical timelines.Radiophysical Characteristics of Lutetium-177A deep understanding of the decay characteristics of $^{177}Lu$ is essential for constructing the mechanistic backbone of any dosimetric refinement model. Lutetium-177 is a rare earth metal that undergoes $\beta^{-}$ decay to stable Hafnium-177 ($^{177}Hf$) with a physical half-life of approximately 6.647 days. This radionuclide is particularly valued in theranostics because its decay involves the emission of both medium-energy electrons for therapeutic effect and gamma photons suitable for diagnostic imaging.Electron Emissions and Local Energy DepositionThe therapeutic component of $^{177}Lu$ consists of $\beta^{-}$ particles and internal conversion electrons. The electron spectrum is characterized by a mean kinetic energy of 147 keV per disintegration and a maximum energy of 497 keV. The spatial distribution of energy deposition is determined by the range of these electrons in human tissue. However, the lower abundance of these photons compared to diagnostic isotopes like Technetium-99m necessitates longer acquisition times or advanced reconstruction techniques to maintain a high signal-to-noise ratio.The Mechanistic Foundation: Local Deposition MethodThe integration of physical knowledge into a SciML model begins with the definition of a mechanistic term, $f_{mech}$, which provides a baseline prediction of the dose map based on established radiobiological formalisms. For $^{177}Lu$, the Local Deposition Method serves as this essential backbone.Formalism and AssumptionsThe LDM simplifies the complex problem of radiation transport by assuming that the energy emitted within a specific volume is deposited locally. This is represented by the equation:
$$D(x, y, z) = \frac{\tilde{A}(x, y, z) \cdot \sum_{i} \Delta_{i} \phi_{i}}{m(x, y, z)}$$
where $D$ is the absorbed dose, $\tilde{A}$ is the time-integrated activity in the voxel, $\Delta_{i}$ is the mean energy emitted per disintegration for emission $i$, $\phi_{i}$ is the absorbed fraction (assumed to be unity for local deposition), and $m$ is the mass of the voxel. The total energy emitted per decay from electrons ($\Delta_{^{177}Lu,e}$) is approximately 147 keV, which is significantly higher—roughly 4.2 to 4.4 times higher—than the total energy emitted per decay from photons ($\Delta_{^{177}Lu,ph}$). Consequently, even a relatively accurate reconstruction of the activity distribution can lead to highly precise dose estimates if the local deposition assumption holds.Density-Based Mass EstimationA critical factor in the LDM is the determination of the voxel mass $m(x, y, z)$, which is derived from the anatomical density map provided by the CT scan. In current clinical implementations, two approaches are common: nominal mass densities and CT-calibration-based densities. Nominal densities assume that organs are homogeneous, whereas CT-based densities use scanner-specific Hounsfield Unit (HU) mapping to determine individual voxel mass.Research comparing these methods has demonstrated that while liver dosimetry is relatively insensitive to the density model (showing differences within 1.4%), lung dosimetry exhibits significant variability. Mean lung doses calculated with CT-based densities are often inflated by as much as 21.3% compared to nominal methods. This inflation is primarily attributed to "stray counts" or system blur that spreads activity from high-density regions into the low-density lung parenchyma, a phenomenon that the UDE's neural term $U_{\theta}$ is uniquely positioned to correct.Advancing CT Calibration: HU to Physical Density MappingTo transition from a qualitative HU map to a physically grounded input for the neural term of the UDE, a robust calibration procedure is required. Standard medical physics protocols utilize piecewise linear mapping to convert HU to either relative electron density (RED) or mass density (MDens). This mapping is essential because the interaction of gamma photons with matter—specifically Compton scattering and the photoelectric effect—is directly dependent on the electron density and atomic number of the tissue.Piecewise Linear Calibration CurvesCalibration is typically performed using phantoms containing inserts of known densities, such as the CIRS 062 phantom. This approach is superior to simple  normalization because it preserves the physical discontinuities at tissue interfaces—such as the soft-tissue-to-lung or soft-tissue-to-bone boundaries—where the most significant attenuation and scatter effects occur.Scientific Machine Learning and the UDE ArchitectureThe refinement of qualitative SPECT into quantitative dosimetry is modeled as a Universal Differential Equation (UDE), a hybrid architecture that combines the strengths of differential equation solvers with the universal approximation capabilities of neural networks. This approach is implemented within the Julia SciML ecosystem, utilizing libraries such as DifferentialEquations.jl, SciMLSensitivity.jl, and Lux.jl.Modeling Refinement as an Initial Value ProblemIn the UDE formulation, the refined dose map $D$ is the state variable of an Initial Value Problem (IVP):$$\frac{dD}{dt} = f_{mech}(A, CT) + U_{\theta}(A, CT, D)$$
The term $f_{mech}$ encodes the Local Deposition Method, while $U_{\theta}$ is a neural residual learner. The integration over "time" $t$ represents the iterative refinement of the dose map, where the initial state $D(0)$ is derived from the uncorrected SPECT reconstruction. By treating the refinement as a continuous dynamical system, we can leverage the sophisticated adjoint sensitivity analysis tools of the Julia ecosystem to compute gradients with respect to the neural network parameters $\theta$ without the memory constraints typical of deep 3D convolutional networks.Lux.jl and Explicit ParameterizationThe choice of Lux.jl as the neural network framework is motivated by its explicit parameter handling. Unlike traditional frameworks like Flux.jl, where parameters are often stored implicitly within the layer objects, Lux.jl separates the model structure from the parameter vector $p$ and state variables $st$. This explicit interface is critical for type promotion and stability in differential equation solvers, particularly when using advanced sensitivities like GaussAdjoint or InterpolatingAdjoint.A typical UDE refinement layer in Lux might be structured as follows:Juliarbf(x) = exp.(-(x.^ 2)) # Radial basis function for smooth gradients

The use of smooth activation functions like RBF or tanh is often preferred in scientific contexts as they provide stable higher-order derivatives, which are necessary for the Jacobian-based explainability analysis.Fourier Neural Operators for Resolution InvarianceOne of the most persistent challenges in medical imaging is the disparity in voxel dimensions between SPECT and CT. Standard convolutional architectures are often resolution-dependent, meaning a model trained on a $128 \times 128 \times 128$ matrix may fail when applied to a $256 \times 256 \times 256$ grid. To address this, the project utilizes Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs) through NeuralOperators.jl.Frequency Domain LearningFNOs learn the mapping between activity and dose in the frequency domain by parameterizing the integral kernel in the Fourier space. This provides the model with resolution invariance, allowing it to generalize across different voxel sizes without retraining. For dosimetry, this is particularly powerful as it enables the model to perform high-resolution refinement even when the input SPECT data is sparsely sampled or reconstructed on a coarse grid. The FNO architecture effectively captures global patterns—such as the overall patient habitus and its influence on photon attenuation—that local convolutional kernels might overlook.Multi-Peak Attenuation Correction StrategiesThe presence of multiple photopeaks in the $^{177}Lu$ emission spectrum (113 keV and 208 keV) necessitates a nuanced approach to attenuation correction. The linear attenuation coefficient $\mu$ is energy-dependent, and using a single "effective" attenuation map based on a weighted mean energy can introduce significant quantification errors.Integrating Peak-Specific CoefficientsIn the UDE's mechanistic term, the integration of peak-specific attenuation coefficients can be achieved through a weighted summation of the linear attenuation maps generated at each energy. Simulation studies have shown that using the attenuation map generated for 190 keV photons (a common approximation) results in calculated calibration factors that are 10% lower than those using a peak-specific approach.The recommended strategy for the UDE mechanistic term is to define an effective linear attenuation map $\mu_{eff}$ as:$$\mu_{eff}(x, y, z) = \alpha \cdot \mu_{113}(x, y, z) + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \mu_{208}(x, y, z)$$
where $\alpha$ is a weighting factor determined by the relative abundance of the peaks and the energy-dependent sensitivity of the SPECT detector. This approach ensures that the "base" attenuation correction is physically grounded for the entire spectrum of emitted photons.Handling Downscatter through Neural ResidualsWhile attenuation is a deterministic process governed by the Beer-Lambert law, Compton scatter—and particularly the downscatter from the 208 keV peak into the 113 keV window—is more complex to model analytically. Roughly 50% of the counts in the 113 keV window are estimated to be scatter events, compared to 20% in the 208 keV window. The neural term $U_{\theta}$ is specifically tasked with learning these scatter residuals. By conditioning $U_{\theta}$ on both the activity map and the high-density bone-tissue boundaries identified in the RED map, the model can learn to subtract the appropriate amount of scatter based on the surrounding anatomical geometry.Conservation of Energy and the "Physics Loss"In dosimetry, a fundamental physical constraint is that the total energy deposited in the recovered dose map must be related to the total activity present in the source organs, adjusted for radioactive decay and biological elimination. Enforcing this constraint is vital to prevent the neural network from over-correcting for local artifacts at the expense of global accuracy.Enforcing Global Energy BalanceThis is achieved by incorporating a "Physics Loss" term into the training objective function. The total loss $\mathcal{L}$ is defined as:
$$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{voxel} + \lambda_{phys} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{physics}$$where $\mathcal{L}_{voxel}$ is a standard Mean Squared Error (MSE) term comparing the predicted dose to a Monte Carlo ground truth, and $\mathcal{L}_{physics}$ enforces the integral conservation. The physics loss term can be formulated as the difference between the integral of the dose map and the total emitted energy:$$\mathcal{L}_{physics} = \left| \int D \, dm - \int \tilde{A} \cdot \Delta_{total} \, dV \right|$$
By setting a high value for the regularization parameter $\lambda_{phys}$, we ensure that the total energy in the system is conserved. This prevents the model from "leaking" energy during the refinement of high-gradient areas, such as the kidney-liver interface.Balancing Local Scatter CorrectionsA critical insight from medical physics is that while total energy is conserved, scatter correction redistributes counts rather than merely scaling them. The UDE structure naturally handles this by allowing $f_{mech}$ to manage the primary energy scaling (via the LDM) and $U_{\theta}$ to manage the local redistribution. The physics loss term should be sufficiently flexible to allow for local variations while ensuring that the global sum remains physically consistent with the injected activity.Explainability: Transparent by DesignFor a clinical model to be trusted by medical physicists and oncologists, it must be "Transparent by Design," allowing for the visualization and verification of its internal logic. The UDE framework provides unique opportunities for such explainability.Visualizing Mechanistic vs. Neural ContributionsBecause the refinement equation is additive, we can explicitly visualize the contribution of the physical term $f_{mech}$ versus the neural correction $U_{\theta}$ for any given patient scan. In regions of homogeneous soft tissue, we expect $f_{mech}$ to contribute nearly 100% of the refinement, as the LDM is highly accurate in such environments. Once the neural network $U_{\theta}$ has been successfully trained, we can apply symbolic regression—using tools like SymbolicRegression.jl—to approximate the neural term with a mathematical formula.This allows for the extraction of a new, interpretable dosimetric equation that combines the LDM with a discovered scatter-correction factor. For example, the symbolic expression might reveal a dependence of the scatter residual on the local gradient of the electron density, providing new insights into the behavior of the $^{177}Lu$ emission spectrum in complex patient geometries. This discovered model can then be implemented in standard clinical workstations without the need for a full deep learning environment, enhancing the scalability of the research.Clinical Verification and Implementation StatusThe proposed SciML framework is currently being validated on a multi-patient cohort from the $^{177}Lu$ dataset. This transition toward personalized molecular radiotherapy, supported by transparent and physically consistent computational models, represents a major milestone in the quest to optimize cancer treatment outcomes.ConclusionThe integration of Scientific Machine Learning with the radiophysical principles of Lutetium-177 offers a transformative solution to the long-standing problem of quantitative SPECT dosimetry. By framing dose refinement as a Universal Differential Equation, we combine the established reliability of the Local Deposition Method with the adaptive power of neural residuals to correct for complex photon interactions. The use of piecewise linear CT calibration to generate relative electron density maps provides the model with a physically grounded manifold, while Fourier Neural Operators ensure resolution invariance across clinical platforms. Furthermore, the enforcement of energy conservation through a physics-informed loss function and the verification of feature sensitivity via Jacobian analysis ensure that the resulting dose maps are both accurate and clinically explainable. As these models move toward broader validation, they lay the foundation for a new era of personalized, dosimetry-guided radionuclide therapy.
